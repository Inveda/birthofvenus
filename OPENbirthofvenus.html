The Birth of Venus

The Birth of Venus is a painting by the Italian artist Sandro Botticelli probably made in the mid-1480s. It depicts the goddess Venus arriving at the shore after her birth, When she had emerged from the sea Fully-grown. 

The Birth of Venus is undoubtedly one of the world’s most famous and appreciated works of art. Painted by Sandro Botticelli between 1482 and 1485, it has become a landmark of XV century Italian painting, so rich in meaning and allegorical references to antiquity.

Ovid’s Metamorphoses

The theme comes from Ovid’s Metamorphoses, a very important oeuvre of the Latin literature. Venus is portrayed naked on a shell on the seashore; on her left the winds blow gently caressing her hair with a shower of roses, on her right a handmaid (Ora) waits for the goddess to go closer to dress her shy body. The meadow is sprinkled with violets, symbol of modesty but often used for love potions.

Botticelli’s most memorable contribution to Renaissance art are his paintings: La Primavera, Venus and Mars, and the Birth of Venus, All in the Uffizi Gallery Florence. All Three contain complex and allegorical meanings. In addition, Boticelli completed some of the best drawings of the Renaissance, and painted numerous tempera frescoes for several Florentine Churches.
Botticelli’s pictures were painted using tempera, a method in which color-pigments are combined with an emulsion of water and egg yolks, or whole eggs. Tempera was commonly used in Florence in the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries, both for panel painting and fresco work, until it was replaced by oil painting. Tempera artist creates lighter or darker shades by adding light or darker dots or lines of color to an area of dried paint. Botticelli painted most of his pictures on wooden panels; though some were executed on canvased and completed many wall paintings.
 

ANALYSIS

 

A unique mythological painting from the renaissance in Florence, and the first non-religious nude since classical antiquity, the birth of Venus belongs to the group of mythological pictures painted by Sandro Botticelli  in the 1480s, following his return from Rome after completing three fresco paintings in the Sistine Chapel for Pope Sixtus IV.

The other mythological works Include Pallas and the centaur , Venus and Mars  and La primavera . Like these works, the Birth of Venus remains one of the profound treasures of the Florentine Renaissance.

The work, Painted with tempera on canvas, depicts the female nude figure of the goddess Venus standing on dry land having emerged from the sea. It was commissioned by Lorenzo the Magnificent of the Medici Family, whose quattrocento humanist circle was particularly interested in classical mythology, and marks the culmination of the revival of ancient myths, within the context if humanistic renaissance art.

A perfect example of the type of picture targeted by the Dominican monk Savonarola, in his virulent 4-yeay campaign against profanity and frivolity, the Painting miraculously survived the monk’s “bonfire of vanities” in 1497. This, despite its obviously pagan narrative and the fact it contained one of the first full length female nudes since the classical era. This good fortune enables us to enjoy one of the greatest renaissance paintings by one of the most stylish of early Renaissance artist.

INTERPRETATIONS

 
The Birth of Venus has attracted a number of other explanations from a variety of scholars, historians, and related experts. The Painting was inspired by a Homeric Hymn published in Florence in 1488 by the Greek Writer Demetrios Chalcondyles; It was Associated with Venus Anadyomene (Venus Rising from the Sea), a lost masterpiece of the artist, Apelles, Mentioned by the classical historian Pliny the Elder
Neoplatonic Interpretation

According to the ancient Greek philosopher Plato, Venus had two aspects: she was an earthly goddess who inspired humans to physical love, but on the other hand she was a celestial goddess who inspired humans to intellectual love. Plato also argued that contemplation of physical beauty enabled the human mind to comprehend spiritual beauty.

This means that when 15th-century Neo-platonic viewers looked at the Birth they would have felt themselves being inspired to contemplate spiritual love. If this sounds somewhat airy-fairy, note that The Birth of Venus and other similar works by Botticelli and other are now being seen as wedding paintings that recommend suitable behaviors for bride and groom. 
Christian Interpretation
The Nudity of Venus echoed that of eve in the Garden of Eden. This has led some commentators to speculate that Venus is a personification of the Christian Church. One should not, for instance, that the title of the Virgin Mary is “stella maris”: star of the sea. Perhaps the sea gives birth to Venus just as the Madonna gives birth to Jesus Christ.
